# 1. Specification and Verification in LTL

Source: *COMP64602: Specification and Verification in LTL*

## 1.1 Verification vs validation

The slides distinguish two related but different questions:

- **Validation:** “Did we build the right system?”
  - This asks whether the system matches the intended real-world need or user requirement.
- **Verification:** “Did we build the system right?”
  - This asks whether the implementation satisfies a formal specification.

### Key concept — verification

**Intuition:** Checking correctness against a formal requirement.

**Formalism in slides:** Not given as a single formal definition, but later model checking is presented as checking whether every possible execution sequence satisfies a formula.

### Key concept — validation

**Intuition:** Checking whether the chosen requirements/system are the right ones.

**Formalism in slides:** No formal definition given.

## 1.5 Model checking: concept

The slides describe model checking as follows:

- Model a program as a transition system.
- Use that transition system to generate execution sequences.
- Let $\Sigma$ be a finite set of execution sequences representing all possible executions of a program or component.
- Then check:

$$
\forall \sigma \in \Sigma . \langle \sigma, s_0\rangle \models \phi
$$

- If model checking fails, it can return the failing sequence as a **counter-example**.
- Notation: for a sequence of states $\sigma$, the set of propositions true at the $i$-th position in the sequence is $\pi(i)$.

### Key concept — model checking

**Intuition:** Exhaustively check every possible execution of a model against a formal property.

**Formal version from slides:**

$$
\forall \sigma \in \Sigma . \langle \sigma, s_0\rangle \models \phi
$$

## 1.6 LTL semantics over sequences of states

**Exam flag:** The slide title explicitly says the LTL semantics are **not examinable**.

The semantics are still useful for understanding the examples.

### Atomic propositions

$$
\langle \sigma, s_i\rangle \models p
\quad \text{iff} \quad
p \in \pi(i)
$$

where $p \in \Pi$.

### Negation

$$
\langle \sigma, s_i\rangle \models \neg \phi
$$

iff it is not the case that:

$$
\langle \sigma, s_i\rangle \models \phi
$$

### Conjunction

$$
\langle \sigma, s_i\rangle \models \phi \land \psi
$$

iff:

$$
\langle \sigma, s_i\rangle \models \phi
$$

and:

$$
\langle \sigma, s_i\rangle \models \psi
$$

### Disjunction

$$
\langle \sigma, s_i\rangle \models \phi \lor \psi
$$

iff:

$$
\langle \sigma, s_i\rangle \models \phi
$$

or:

$$
\langle \sigma, s_i\rangle \models \psi
$$

### Next

$$
\langle \sigma, s_i\rangle \models \bigcirc \phi
\quad \text{iff} \quad
\langle \sigma, s_{i+1}\rangle \models \phi
$$

### Eventually

$$
\langle \sigma, s_i\rangle \models \Diamond \phi
$$

iff there exists $j$ such that:

$$
j \geq i
$$

and:

$$
\langle \sigma, s_j\rangle \models \phi
$$

### Always

$$
\langle \sigma, s_i\rangle \models \Box \phi
$$

iff for all $j$, if $j \geq i$, then:

$$
\langle \sigma, s_j\rangle \models \phi
$$

### Until

$$
\langle \sigma, s_i\rangle \models \phi \, U \, \psi
$$

iff there exists $j$ such that:

$$
j \geq i
$$

and:

$$
\langle \sigma, s_j\rangle \models \psi
$$

and for all $k$, if:

$$
j > k \geq i
$$

then:

$$
\langle \sigma, s_k\rangle \models \phi
$$

The slide notes that $\Box$ and $\Diamond$ can be expressed using $U$ and $\neg$.

## 1.7 Worked example 1: checking a true LTL property

The sequence shown is:

$$
c \to b \to a \to \cdots \to e
$$

The property to check is:

$$
\langle \sigma, s_0\rangle \models c \land \bigcirc b \land \bigcirc\bigcirc a
$$

By conjunction, this reduces to three checks:

1. Check:

$$
\langle \sigma, s_0\rangle \models c
$$

which means:

$$
c \in \pi(0)
$$

2. Check:

$$
\langle \sigma, s_0\rangle \models \bigcirc b
$$

which means:

$$
b \in \pi(1)
$$

3. Check:

$$
\langle \sigma, s_0\rangle \models \bigcirc\bigcirc a
$$

which means:

$$
a \in \pi(2)
$$

Since the sequence has $c$ at position 0, $b$ at position 1, and $a$ at position 2, the formula is satisfied.

## 1.8 Worked example 2: same sequence, different property

The same sequence is used:

$$
c \to b \to a \to \cdots \to e
$$

The property to check is:

$$
\langle \sigma, s_0\rangle \models c \land \bigcirc c \land \bigcirc\bigcirc \neg a
$$

By conjunction, this reduces to:

1. Check:

$$
\langle \sigma, s_0\rangle \models c
$$

which means:

$$
c \in \pi(0)
$$

This is true.

2. Check:

$$
\langle \sigma, s_0\rangle \models \bigcirc c
$$

which means:

$$
c \in \pi(1)
$$

This is not true, because position 1 contains $b$, not $c$.

3. Check:

$$
\langle \sigma, s_0\rangle \models \bigcirc\bigcirc \neg a
$$

which means:

$$
a \notin \pi(2)
$$

This is not true, because position 2 contains $a$.

Therefore the whole conjunction is false.

# 2. CTL, ATL, and other logics

Source: *COMP64602: CTL, ATL and Other Logics*

## 2.1 Why CTL/CTL*?

In LTL, formulas have to be true on **all paths** through the model. Sometimes we want to talk about truth on just **some paths**.

**Computational Tree Logic / CTL\*** introduces path quantifiers:

- $A$: for all paths.
- $E$: there exists a path.

A property containing a temporal operator must have a path quantifier somewhere before the temporal operator.

Any LTL formula $\phi$ translates into CTL* as:

$$
A\phi
$$

That is, the LTL-style “true over all paths” interpretation becomes explicit in CTL*.

---

## 2.2 CTL* semantics

**Exam flag:** The slide title explicitly says **CTL\* semantics are not examinable**.

The slides state that CTL* extends LTL semantics “in the obvious way.”

### Atomic proposition

$$
\langle M, s_i\rangle \models p
\quad \text{iff} \quad
p \in \pi(i)
$$

for $p \in \Pi$.

### Until

$$
\langle M, \sigma\rangle \models \phi U \psi
$$

iff there exists $j$ such that:

$$
s_j \in \sigma
$$

$$
j \geq 0
$$

$$
\langle M, s_j\rangle \models \psi
$$

and for all $k$, if:

$$
s_k \in \sigma
$$

and:

$$
j > k \geq 0
$$

then:

$$
\langle M, s_k\rangle \models \phi
$$

### Universal path quantifier

$$
\langle M, s_i\rangle \models A\phi
$$

iff:

$$
\langle M, \sigma\rangle \models \phi
$$

for all paths $\sigma$ starting at $s_i$.

### Existential path quantifier

$$
\langle M, s_i\rangle \models E\phi
$$

iff:

$$
\langle M, \sigma\rangle \models \phi
$$

for some path $\sigma$ starting at $s_i$.

---

## 2.3 CTL as a fragment of CTL*

CTL is a fragment of CTL* where every temporal operator, such as:

$$
\Box
$$

or:

$$
\Diamond
$$

must be immediately preceded by a path quantifier.

### CTL formula example

$$
A \Box E \bigcirc red
$$

Meaning given on the slide: on all paths it is always the case that there exists a path where $red$ is true in the next step.

This is a CTL formula because the temporal operators are immediately paired with path quantifiers.

### Not a CTL formula

$$
A \Box \Diamond red
$$

Meaning given on the slide: on all paths it is always the case that eventually $red$ will be true.

This is **not** CTL because the $\Diamond$ is not immediately preceded by a path quantifier.

The slides state that CTL has better computational properties than CTL*, so CTL is preferable if automated reasoning is required.

## 2.5 Alternating Time Temporal Logic: ATL*

In multi-agent systems, we often want to talk about whether groups of agents can work together to achieve something.

ATL* generalises CTL by replacing path quantifiers $A$ and $E$ with **cooperation modalities**:

$$
\langle\langle A\rangle\rangle
$$

where $A$ is a team of agents.

The formula:

$$
\langle\langle A\rangle\rangle \phi
$$

expresses that the team of agents $A$ has the power to ensure that $\phi$ becomes true.

As with CTL*, a property containing a temporal operator must have a cooperation modality before it. In ATL, the cooperation modality must immediately precede the temporal operator.

### Key concept — cooperation modality

**Intuition:** A way of saying “this coalition has a strategy to force this outcome.”

**Formalism from slides:**

$$
\langle\langle A\rangle\rangle \phi
$$

meaning team $A$ can ensure $\phi$.

---

## 2.6 ATL* high-level semantics

The ATL* semantics are explained using concurrent agent actions.

In any state:

- Consider all actions the agents can take.
- There is a transition function:

$$
\tau
$$

which takes:

$$
s
$$

and a tuple of actions:

$$
(\alpha_1,\ldots,\alpha_n)
$$

and returns the next state.

Here:

$$
\alpha_i
$$

is the action taken by agent $a_i$.

There is also an action function:

$$
d
$$

which defines the actions available to each agent in each state.

For:

$$
\tau(s,\alpha_1,\ldots,\alpha_n)
$$

to be valid, each chosen action must be available:

$$
\alpha_i \in d(a_i,s)
$$

This structure is called a **concurrent game structure**.

### Example ATL properties

#### Property 1

“If the carriage is in position 0, $R1$ can ensure that it is never in position 1.”

$$
pos0 \to \langle\langle R1\rangle\rangle \Box \neg pos1

#### Property 2

$$
pos0 \to \neg \langle\langle R2\rangle\rangle \bigcirc pos1
$$

# 3. Modular Interpreted Systems

Source: *COMP64602: Modular Interpreted Systems*

## 3.1 Model checking: basic idea

The lecture restates the model-checking method:

1. Represent the system as some kind of transition system, for example a concurrent game structure.
2. Represent the negation of the property as another transition system.
3. The negated-property transition system represents “bad paths” through the system model.
4. Combine the two.
5. Check whether the combination is empty.
6. If the combination is empty, no bad path is possible in the system model.

### Key concept — bad path

**Intuition:** A path through the model that violates the property being checked.

**Formalism from slides:** The negation of the property is represented as a transition system whose paths are bad paths.

---

## 3.3 Why not input the full system model directly?

The lecture says we could input the full concurrent game structure, but there are problems:

- Full CGSs may be large.
- They may be difficult to construct.
- We often know more about individual agents than about the combined system.
- We might need to represent partial knowledge: some agents may not know the full global state.

This motivates modular interpreted systems.

## 3.4 Modular Interpreted Systems: core idea

A Modular Interpreted System is:

- An implicit representation of the system.
- It can be unfolded during model checking to build the system model.
- It is a compact high-level representation.
- It is based on:
  - individual agents,
  - their information,
  - and how they act.
- It builds a model of the multi-agent system by unfolding the actions all agents take concurrently.

The slide explicitly says the course does **not** require mastery of the full complexity of MIS, only the key ideas in simplified form.

### Key concept — MIS

**Intuition:** Instead of manually building one giant global transition system, describe each agent locally and let the combined model unfold from their concurrent actions.

**Formalism from slides:** The individual-agent representation is given next.

---

## 3.5 Agent representation

**Exam flag:** The slide title says: “You will be given this in the exam if it is needed.”

Each individual agent is represented as a transition system:

$$
P_i = \langle St_i, d_i, o_i, \Pi_i, \pi_i\rangle
$$

where:

- $St_i$: the set of states the agent may be in.
- $d_i$: a function that returns the actions the agent may take in any state.
- $o_i$: the internal transition function; it takes the current state and the actions of all agents, then returns the next step.
- $\Pi_i$: the agent’s propositions.
- $\pi_i$: the valuation function.

---

## 3.6 Building the multi-agent system from agents

The system state is a tuple of the states of the agents, possibly also including the environment:

$$
s = \langle s_1,\ldots,s_n,s_{env}\rangle
$$

The valuation function is defined as:

$$
s \in \pi(p)
\quad \text{iff} \quad
s_i \in \pi_i(p)
$$

for all $i$.

[UNCLEAR] The slide says “for all $i$.” It does not clarify how this works for propositions belonging only to a particular agent.

The set of available action combinations is:

$$
d(s) =
\{
\langle \alpha_1,\ldots,\alpha_n\rangle
\mid
\alpha_i \in d_i(s_i)
\}
$$

To build a transition:

1. Pick:

$$
\alpha \in d(s)
$$

2. For model checking, explore all possible $\alpha$.
3. The next state is:

$$
\langle
o_1(s_1,\alpha),
\ldots,
o_n(s_n,\alpha),
o_{env}(s_{env},\alpha)
\rangle
$$

---

## 3.7 Perfect and imperfect information

### Perfect information

An agent has perfect information if it can tell apart every global state.

The slide describes the global state as:

$$
\langle s_1,\ldots,s_n,s_{env}\rangle
$$

With perfect information, the agent’s possible states align with the set of global states.

### Imperfect information

An agent has imperfect information if some global states look the same to the agent.

For model checking, this gives an equivalence relation for each agent. If:

$$
s_1 \sim_i s_2
$$

then agent $i$ cannot tell these states apart when choosing an action.

[UNCLEAR] The slide’s notation around $\sim_i$ is garbled in the parsed text, but the intended idea is clear: indistinguishability is represented by an equivalence relation.

---

## 3.8 Perfect and imperfect recall

### Perfect recall

Agents have perfect recall if they can choose their actions based on the entire history of states and actions up to the current point.

### Imperfect recall

Agents have imperfect recall if they base their decisions only on the current state.

---

## 3.9 ATL* and strategies in MIS

ATL* lets us reason about strategies available to agents, given the actions available to them.

Variations of ATL* allow reasoning based on whether agents have:

- perfect or imperfect information,
- perfect or imperfect recall.

This makes ATL* and MIS useful for reasoning about the potential behaviour of a multi-agent system without knowing exactly how the agents are implemented.

The slides note that deployed agents still follow some particular strategy, which may be implemented or learned in different ways.

# 4. Model-checking agent programming languages / MCAPL

Source: *COMP64602: Model-Checking Agent Programming Languages*

## 4.1 Gwendolen reasoning cycle

The lecture recalls that Gwendolen has a reasoning cycle.

The diagram gives these stages:

### Stage A

Select a current intention or sleep the agent.

Decision point:

- Are there any unsuspended, non-empty intentions?
  - If yes: proceed to Stage B.
  - If no: proceed toward perception/message handling.

### Stage B

Find all plans applicable to the current intention.

### Stage C

Pick a plan and apply it.

Decision point:

- Is the current intention empty?
  - If yes: move to perception/message handling.
  - If no: execute the top deed.

### Stage D

Execute the top deed on the current intention.

### Stage E

Get new perception and messages.

### Stage F

Process new messages.

Then the cycle returns to Stage A.

### Key concept — reasoning cycle

**Intuition:** The agent repeatedly chooses what it is trying to do, finds applicable plans, applies a plan, executes deeds, observes the environment, and handles messages.

**Formalism from slides:** Given as the Stage A–F flow diagram rather than a mathematical definition.

---

## 4.2 Operational semantics

Gwendolen has an operational semantics that determines execution of the reasoning cycle.

The slides define the idea of operational semantics as follows:

- It represents the state of a program as a tuple of data structures.
- Examples of those structures include:
  - beliefs,
  - goals,
  - intentions.
- Instructions in the program cause transitions on this state.
- The operational semantics is implemented using the Agent Infrastructure Layer, AIL.
- Several other languages in the MCAPL framework are also implemented using AIL.

### Key concept — operational semantics

**Intuition:** A precise account of how each instruction changes the program state.

**Formalism from slides:** Program state is represented as a tuple of structures; commands define transitions over that tuple.

---

## 4.3 Worked example: adding a belief

Suppose the program state consists of:

- belief base:

$$
\mathcal B
$$

- goal base:

$$
\mathcal G
$$

The current command is to add a belief:

$$
+b
$$

Then the transition is:

$$
\langle \mathcal B, \mathcal G\rangle
\xrightarrow{+b}
\langle \mathcal B \cup \{b\}, \mathcal G\rangle
$$

So the new state is the same as the old state except that the belief base has been updated to include $b$. The goal base is unchanged.

The slide notes that it is natural to do model checking over this kind of operational transition system.

# 5. Cross-lecture connections

## 5.1 LTL → CTL/CTL*

The LTL lecture begins with formulas over sequences of states. The CTL/ATL lecture then extends this by explaining that LTL properties are interpreted over all paths, while CTL* makes path quantification explicit using $A$ and $E$. Any LTL formula $\phi$ can be translated as $A\phi$ in CTL*.

## 5.2 CTL/ATL → MIS

The CTL/ATL lecture introduces concurrent game structures using states, joint actions, and transition functions. MIS then uses the same multi-agent motivation but focuses on representing the system modularly through individual agents, their actions, and their information.

## 5.3 MIS → strategic reasoning

MIS supports reasoning about possible behaviour without knowing the exact implementation of each agent. ATL* is the logic used to reason about strategies, including variations based on perfect/imperfect information and perfect/imperfect recall.

## 5.4 Operational semantics → program model checking

The MCAPL lecture shifts from abstract transition systems to actual agent programming languages. Gwendolen programs have operational semantics, and program instructions cause transitions in program state. This makes it natural to apply model checking directly to the program execution model.

## 5.5 Agent properties beyond ordinary temporal logic

The final lectures move beyond plain temporal properties into properties involving:

- beliefs,
- goals,
- actions/deeds,
- commitments,
- agent strategies,
- protocols.

Examples include:

$$
B(ag1, obstacle) \to \bigcirc G(ag1, stop)
$$

and:

$$
\Diamond B(ag2, win)
$$

These are richer than ordinary LTL/CTL because they refer to agent mental attitudes or agent-specific events.
