<summary><strong>Q1.</strong> How do you recognize a propositional variable in this course’s PL?</summary>

Use: Treat it as an atomic yes/no statement; do not look inside it for objects, arguments, time, or probability.<br>Step 1: Check whether the symbol is in the chosen variable set 𝒫.<br>Step 2: If it is in 𝒫, it can be assigned 0/false or 1/true by a valuation.<br>Step 3: If it has predicate structure like P(x), quantifiers, or temporal/probabilistic operators, it is not a propositional variable in basic PL.<br><b>Reference:</b> Propositional variables are elements of 𝒫 and represent atomic statements with truth values in {0,1}.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Q2.</strong> How do you test whether an expression is a propositional formula under the primitive syntax?</summary>

Discriminator: Can it be generated from 𝒫 using only ¬, ∧, ∨, and parentheses?<br>Step 1: Mark every variable p ∈ 𝒫 as a formula.<br>Step 2: If φ is a formula, then ¬φ is a formula.<br>Step 3: If φ and ψ are formulae, then φ ∧ ψ and φ ∨ ψ are formulae.<br>Step 4: Reject anything requiring operators or structures not generated by these rules.<br><b>Reference:</b> The formulae over 𝒫 are the smallest set closed under variables, negation, conjunction, and disjunction.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Q3.</strong> Why does “smallest set” matter in the inductive syntax definition?</summary>

Use: It prevents you from accepting extra expressions just because they look logical.<br>Step 1: Start only with the base cases: variables in 𝒫.<br>Step 2: Add only expressions obtained by the allowed construction rules.<br>Step 3: Do not add implication, quantifiers, predicates, or other operators unless they have been explicitly defined.<br><b>Reference:</b> “Smallest” means no expression counts as a formula unless it is forced by the inductive rules.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Q4.</strong> How do you read the primitive PL connectives?</summary>

Use: Translate symbols into their truth-functional role before manipulating them.<br>Step 1: Read ¬φ as “not φ”.<br>Step 2: Read φ ∧ ψ as “φ and ψ”.<br>Step 3: Read φ ∨ ψ as “φ or ψ”.<br><b>Reference:</b> The primitive connectives in this sheet are negation ¬, conjunction ∧, and disjunction ∨.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Q5.</strong> Discriminator: am I dealing with syntax or semantics?</summary>

Ask: Am I checking legal form, or truth/meaning?<br>Syntax: asks whether an expression is well-formed according to the construction rules.<br>Semantics: asks what truth value the formula has under valuations.<br><b>Reference:</b> Syntax defines what can be written; semantics defines what formulae mean.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Q6.</strong> How do you use a valuation?</summary>

Use: Treat it as a complete truth assignment for propositional variables.<br>Step 1: Choose a truth value for each p ∈ 𝒫.<br>Step 2: Write v(p)=0 or v(p)=1 for each variable.<br>Step 3: Extend v from variables to compound formulae using the semantic clauses.<br><b>Reference:</b> A valuation is a mapping v: 𝒫 → {0,1}.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Q7.</strong> How is a valuation extended to negation?</summary>

Use: Flip the truth value of the subformula.<br>Step 1: Evaluate v(φ).<br>Step 2: Compute 1 − v(φ).<br>Step 3: Assign that value to v(¬φ).<br><b>Reference:</b> v(¬φ) := 1 − v(φ).

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Q8.</strong> How is a valuation extended to conjunction?</summary>

Use: Require both sides to be true.<br>Step 1: Evaluate v(φ).<br>Step 2: Evaluate v(ψ).<br>Step 3: Multiply the two values.<br><b>Reference:</b> v(φ ∧ ψ) := v(φ) · v(ψ), so the result is 1 iff both sides are 1.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Q9.</strong> How is a valuation extended to disjunction?</summary>

Use: Require at least one side to be true.<br>Step 1: Evaluate v(φ).<br>Step 2: Evaluate v(ψ).<br>Step 3: Take the maximum of the two values.<br><b>Reference:</b> v(φ ∨ ψ) := max(v(φ), v(ψ)), so the result is 1 iff at least one side is 1.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Q10.</strong> How do you evaluate a compound PL formula under a valuation?</summary>

Step 1: Identify the innermost subformulae.<br>Step 2: Evaluate variables using v.<br>Step 3: Evaluate ¬, ∧, and ∨ using their semantic clauses.<br>Step 4: Work outward until the whole formula has value 0 or 1.<br>Step 5: Conclude true if the final value is 1, false if it is 0.<br><b>Reference:</b> A valuation makes φ true iff v(φ)=1 and false iff v(φ)=0.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Q11.</strong> How do you decide whether a valuation makes a formula true or false?</summary>

Step 1: Evaluate the formula under v.<br>Step 2: If the final value is 1, v makes the formula true.<br>Step 3: If the final value is 0, v makes the formula false.<br><b>Reference:</b> v makes φ true iff v(φ)=1; v makes φ false iff v(φ)=0.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Q12.</strong> What is the safe way to use partial-looking information in PL semantics?</summary>

Discriminator: Is this formal semantics or an algorithmic shortcut?<br>Formal semantics: use a full valuation assigning every variable in 𝒫.<br>Algorithmic reasoning: you may sometimes track only literals relevant to a branch.<br><b>Reference:</b> The semantic definition uses full valuations v: 𝒫 → {0,1}.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Q13.</strong> How do you build a truth table for a formula?</summary>

Step 1: List all propositional variables occurring in the formula.<br>Step 2: Create one row for each full valuation of those variables.<br>Step 3: Add columns for useful subformulae.<br>Step 4: Fill subformula columns from smallest to largest using the semantic clauses.<br>Step 5: Read the final column to answer reasoning questions.<br><b>Reference:</b> A truth table summarises valuations; with n variables it has 2^n rows.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Q14.</strong> How do you read satisfiability from a truth table?</summary>

Discriminator: Is there at least one final-column 1?<br>Step 1: Look at the final column for φ.<br>Step 2: If any row has value 1, φ is satisfiable.<br>Step 3: If every row has value 0, φ is unsatisfiable.<br><b>Reference:</b> φ is satisfiable iff ∃ valuation v such that v(φ)=1.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Q15.</strong> How do you read validity from a truth table?</summary>

Discriminator: Is every final-column entry 1?<br>Step 1: Look at the final column for φ.<br>Step 2: If every row has value 1, φ is valid.<br>Step 3: If any row has value 0, φ is not valid.<br><b>Reference:</b> φ is valid iff ∀ valuations v, v(φ)=1.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Q16.</strong> Discriminator: satisfiable or valid?</summary>

Ask: true under at least one valuation, or true under every valuation?<br>Satisfiable: one successful valuation is enough.<br>Valid: no counterexample valuation is allowed.<br>Mixed final column: satisfiable but not valid.<br><b>Reference:</b> Satisfiable means ∃v v(φ)=1; valid means ∀v v(φ)=1.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Q17.</strong> How do you test equivalence using a truth table?</summary>

Step 1: Build or compare final columns for φ and ψ under the same valuations.<br>Step 2: If every row gives the same truth value for both, they are equivalent.<br>Step 3: If any row differs, they are not equivalent.<br><b>Reference:</b> φ ≡ ψ iff ∀v, v(φ)=v(ψ).

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Q18.</strong> Discriminator: equivalence or syntactic identity?</summary>

Ask: Are the formulae the same string, or do they always have the same truth value?<br>Syntactic identity: same written expression.<br>Equivalence: same truth value under every valuation, even if written differently.<br><b>Reference:</b> φ ≡ ψ iff ∀v, v(φ)=v(ψ).

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Q19.</strong> How do you test entailment using truth values?</summary>

Discriminator: Is there a counterexample with premise true and conclusion false?<br>Step 1: Consider valuations where φ is true.<br>Step 2: Check whether ψ is also true in all of them.<br>Step 3: If any valuation has v(φ)=1 and v(ψ)=0, entailment fails.<br>Step 4: If no such valuation exists, entailment holds.<br><b>Reference:</b> φ → ψ iff ∀v, v(φ) ≤ v(ψ).

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Q20.</strong> How do you test entailment using a truth table?</summary>

Step 1: Build columns for φ and ψ under the same valuations.<br>Step 2: Search for a row where φ=1 and ψ=0.<br>Step 3: If such a row exists, it is a counterexample and φ does not entail ψ.<br>Step 4: If no such row exists, φ entails ψ.<br><b>Reference:</b> Entailment excludes exactly the case v(φ)=1 and v(ψ)=0.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Q21.</strong> Discriminator: semantic entailment → or object-language implication ⇒?</summary>

Ask: Is this a relation between formulae, or a formula inside the language?<br>φ → ψ: semantic claim that all φ-worlds are ψ-worlds.<br>φ ⇒ ψ: syntactic abbreviation for a formula.<br><b>Reference:</b> φ → ψ iff φ ⇒ ψ is valid, where φ ⇒ ψ := ¬φ ∨ ψ.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Q22.</strong> How do you expand implication as syntactic sugar?</summary>

Step 1: Identify the antecedent φ and consequent ψ.<br>Step 2: Replace φ ⇒ ψ with ¬φ ∨ ψ.<br>Step 3: Continue reasoning using only ¬, ∧, and ∨.<br><b>Reference:</b> φ ⇒ ψ := ¬φ ∨ ψ.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Q23.</strong> How do you expand biconditional as syntactic sugar?</summary>

Step 1: Replace φ ⇔ ψ by two implications: φ ⇒ ψ and ψ ⇒ φ.<br>Step 2: Conjoin them.<br>Step 3: If needed, expand each implication using ¬ and ∨.<br><b>Reference:</b> φ ⇔ ψ := (φ ⇒ ψ) ∧ (ψ ⇒ φ).

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Q24.</strong> How can ∧ be defined using only ∨ and ¬?</summary>

Step 1: Negate each conjunct.<br>Step 2: Disjoin the negated conjuncts.<br>Step 3: Negate the whole disjunction.<br><b>Reference:</b> φ ∧ ψ ≡ ¬(¬φ ∨ ¬ψ).

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Q25.</strong> How can ∨ be defined using only ∧ and ¬?</summary>

Step 1: Negate each disjunct.<br>Step 2: Conjoin the negated disjuncts.<br>Step 3: Negate the whole conjunction.<br><b>Reference:</b> φ ∨ ψ ≡ ¬(¬φ ∧ ¬ψ).

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Q26.</strong> How do you eliminate double negation?</summary>

Step 1: Find any subformula of the form ¬¬φ.<br>Step 2: Replace it by φ.<br>Step 3: Repeat wherever it occurs.<br><b>Reference:</b> ¬¬φ ≡ φ.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Q27.</strong> What should a good KR system description satisfy?</summary>

Discriminator: Is it broken, vacuous, or informative?<br>Step 1: Check that the system formula φ is satisfiable; otherwise the theory is inconsistent.<br>Step 2: Check that φ is not valid; otherwise it constrains nothing.<br>Step 3: Treat satisfiable but not valid as the useful target shape.<br><b>Reference:</b> A meaningful system description should be satisfiable and not valid.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Q28.</strong> How do you check a desirable property of a KR system?</summary>

Step 1: Represent the system as φ and the desired property as ψ.<br>Step 2: Check whether φ entails ψ.<br>Step 3: If φ → ψ holds, every allowed system state has the desired property.<br>Step 4: If it fails, strengthen or fix the KB/formulae.<br><b>Reference:</b> Desired property check: φ → ψ, equivalently validity of φ ⇒ ψ.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Q29.</strong> How do you check an undesirable property of a KR system?</summary>

Step 1: Represent the system as φ and the bad property as ψ.<br>Step 2: Test satisfiability of φ ∧ ψ.<br>Step 3: If φ ∧ ψ is unsatisfiable, the bad situation is ruled out.<br>Step 4: If it is satisfiable, a bad model exists and the KB may need fixing.<br><b>Reference:</b> Undesired property check: φ ∧ ψ should be unsatisfiable.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Q30.</strong> What is a reasoner?</summary>

Use: Identify the reasoning task, then view the software as a yes/no decision tool for that task.<br>Step 1: Specify the input formulae.<br>Step 2: Specify the target task: satisfiability, validity, entailment, or equivalence.<br>Step 3: Return the corresponding yes/no answer.<br><b>Reference:</b> A reasoner is software that solves a reasoning task.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Q31.</strong> How do you decide validity using a SAT solver?</summary>

Step 1: Given φ, form ¬φ.<br>Step 2: Ask the SAT solver whether ¬φ is satisfiable.<br>Step 3: If SAT says yes, φ has a counterexample and is not valid.<br>Step 4: If SAT says no, φ is valid.<br><b>Reference:</b> φ valid iff ¬φ is not satisfiable.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Q32.</strong> How do you decide satisfiability using a validity checker?</summary>

Step 1: Given φ, form ¬φ.<br>Step 2: Ask whether ¬φ is valid.<br>Step 3: If ¬φ is valid, φ is unsatisfiable.<br>Step 4: If ¬φ is not valid, φ is satisfiable.<br><b>Reference:</b> φ satisfiable iff ¬φ is not valid.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Q33.</strong> How do you decide entailment using validity?</summary>

Step 1: Given φ and ψ, form the implication formula φ ⇒ ψ.<br>Step 2: Check whether φ ⇒ ψ is valid.<br>Step 3: If it is valid, φ entails ψ.<br>Step 4: If it is not valid, entailment fails.<br><b>Reference:</b> φ → ψ iff φ ⇒ ψ is valid.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Q34.</strong> How do you decide entailment using a SAT solver?</summary>

Step 1: Given φ and ψ, form φ ∧ ¬ψ.<br>Step 2: Ask whether φ ∧ ¬ψ is satisfiable.<br>Step 3: If SAT says yes, the satisfying valuation is a counterexample and entailment fails.<br>Step 4: If SAT says no, no counterexample exists and entailment holds.<br><b>Reference:</b> φ → ψ iff φ ∧ ¬ψ is not satisfiable.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Q35.</strong> Why can one reasoner support several reasoning tasks?</summary>

Use: Reduce the task you want to the task your solver already handles.<br>Step 1: Express validity via unsatisfiability of negation.<br>Step 2: Express entailment via validity of implication or unsatisfiability of counterexample.<br>Step 3: Swap yes/no answers where the reduction requires it.<br><b>Reference:</b> Satisfiability, validity, and entailment are inter-reducible for PL.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Q36.</strong> Why do truth tables not scale as a reasoning method?</summary>

Step 1: Count the number n of variables.<br>Step 2: Observe that every variable doubles the number of valuations.<br>Step 3: The table has 2^n rows, so enumeration grows exponentially.<br><b>Reference:</b> Truth tables decide PL tasks conceptually, but are infeasible for large formulae because they enumerate all valuations.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Q37.</strong> What is the goal of a propositional calculus/tableau method here?</summary>

Use: Replace blind enumeration with a goal-directed satisfiability procedure.<br>Step 1: Start with a formula to be made true.<br>Step 2: Decompose it according to its connectives.<br>Step 3: Branch only where choices are needed.<br>Step 4: Look for at least one consistent branch.<br><b>Reference:</b> The tableau algorithm is a calculus/algorithm for deciding PL satisfiability.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Q38.</strong> What is the intuition behind analytical tableau?</summary>

Use: Think “reasoning by cases”.<br>Step 1: Break complex formulae into simpler requirements.<br>Step 2: For conjunctions, keep all requirements together.<br>Step 3: For disjunctions, explore alternative cases.<br>Step 4: A successful case gives a valuation making the input true.<br><b>Reference:</b> Tableau tests satisfiability by decomposing formulae and searching for a clash-free branch.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Q39.</strong> How should you interpret 𝒮 in the set-of-sets tableau presentation?</summary>

Step 1: Treat 𝒮 as the collection of current possible branches.<br>Step 2: Treat each S ∈ 𝒮 as one possible set of formulae that should be true together.<br>Step 3: Apply rules by modifying or replacing members of 𝒮.<br><b>Reference:</b> 𝒮 is a set of sets of formulae; each S ∈ 𝒮 represents one possible path/valuation attempt.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Q40.</strong> How do you recognize Negation Normal Form?</summary>

Discriminator: Does every negation sit directly in front of a propositional variable?<br>Step 1: Scan every ¬ symbol.<br>Step 2: If each ¬ has the form ¬p for some variable p, the formula is in NNF.<br>Step 3: If any ¬ applies to a compound formula, it is not in NNF.<br><b>Reference:</b> A formula is in NNF iff negation occurs only in front of propositional variables.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Q41.</strong> Why transform a formula into NNF before tableau?</summary>

Use: Simplify the tableau rules.<br>Step 1: Push negations inward until only literals are negated.<br>Step 2: Then the tableau only needs ∧- and ∨-decomposition rules plus clash checks on literals.<br><b>Reference:</b> NNF makes tableau satisfiability checking simpler by restricting negation to literals.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Q42.</strong> How do you convert a formula to NNF?</summary>

Step 1: Expand any syntactic sugar so only ¬, ∧, and ∨ remain.<br>Step 2: Replace ¬(φ ∧ ψ) by ¬φ ∨ ¬ψ.<br>Step 3: Replace ¬(φ ∨ ψ) by ¬φ ∧ ¬ψ.<br>Step 4: Replace ¬¬φ by φ.<br>Step 5: Repeat until no negation applies to a compound formula.<br><b>Reference:</b> Exhaustive NNF rewriting yields an equivalent formula in NNF.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Q43.</strong> What does the NNF transformation lemma guarantee?</summary>

Use: After exhaustive rewriting, you may reason with the transformed formula instead of the original.<br>Step 1: Check the rewrite rules have been applied until none remain.<br>Step 2: Confirm the result is in NNF.<br>Step 3: Preserve reasoning answers because the result is equivalent to the input.<br><b>Reference:</b> Exhaustive NNF rewriting transforms every PL formula into an equivalent formula in NNF, with no formula-size blow-up beyond linear size in the lecture note.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Q44.</strong> What are the input and output of the tableau SAT algorithm?</summary>

Use: Feed it an NNF formula and read a yes/no satisfiability answer.<br>Input: a PL formula φ in NNF.<br>Output: yes if φ is satisfiable; no otherwise.<br><b>Reference:</b> Tableau is presented as a decision method for PL satisfiability on NNF inputs.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Q45.</strong> How do you initialise the set-of-sets tableau?</summary>

Step 1: Put the input formula φ into a singleton set {φ}.<br>Step 2: Put that singleton set into 𝒮.<br>Step 3: Remember: the outer set stores branches; the inner set stores formulae on one branch.<br><b>Reference:</b> Initialise 𝒮 := {{φ}}.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Q46.</strong> When do you apply the tableau ∧-rule?</summary>

Discriminator: Does a branch contain φ₁ ∧ φ₂ without already containing both conjuncts?<br>Step 1: Pick S ∈ 𝒮 containing φ₁ ∧ φ₂.<br>Step 2: If φ₁ and φ₂ are not both already in S, add both to S.<br>Step 3: Do not branch.<br><b>Reference:</b> ∧-rule: S := S ∪ {φ₁, φ₂}.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Q47.</strong> When do you apply the tableau ∨-rule?</summary>

Discriminator: Does a branch contain φ₁ ∨ φ₂ without already choosing either disjunct?<br>Step 1: Pick S ∈ 𝒮 containing φ₁ ∨ φ₂.<br>Step 2: If neither φ₁ nor φ₂ is in S, replace S by two sets.<br>Step 3: One new set adds φ₁; the other adds φ₂.<br><b>Reference:</b> ∨-rule: replace S by S ∪ {φ₁} and S ∪ {φ₂}.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Q48.</strong> How do you detect a clash in a tableau branch?</summary>

Step 1: Inspect a set S for literals.<br>Step 2: If for some variable p both p and ¬p occur in S, mark a clash.<br>Step 3: If no such pair occurs, S is clash-free.<br><b>Reference:</b> S contains a clash iff {p, ¬p} ⊆ S for some propositional variable p.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Q49.</strong> How do you run the tableau algorithm for satisfiability?</summary>

Step 1: Convert the input formula to NNF if needed.<br>Step 2: Initialise 𝒮 := {{φ}}.<br>Step 3: Apply ∧-rules by adding both conjuncts to a branch.<br>Step 4: Apply ∨-rules by splitting a branch into alternatives.<br>Step 5: Continue until no rule applies.<br>Step 6: Return yes iff at least one final set is clash-free.<br><b>Reference:</b> Tableau decides satisfiability by searching for a clash-free final branch.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Q50.</strong> What is the tableau stopping and return condition?</summary>

Discriminator: After saturation, is there at least one clash-free set?<br>Step 1: Stop when no ∧- or ∨-rule can be applied.<br>Step 2: If some final S ∈ 𝒮 is clash-free, return yes.<br>Step 3: If every final S has a clash, return no.<br><b>Reference:</b> The return condition is existential: one clash-free final set suffices for satisfiability.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Q51.</strong> How do you extract a valuation from a clash-free tableau branch?</summary>

Step 1: For each literal p in S, set v(p)=1.<br>Step 2: For each literal ¬p in S, set v(p)=0.<br>Step 3: For variables not mentioned in S, choose either 0 or 1.<br>Step 4: This is well-defined because S is clash-free.<br><b>Reference:</b> A clash-free final branch can be converted into a valuation satisfying all formulae on that branch.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Q52.</strong> Discriminator: set-of-sets tableau or tree tableau?</summary>

Ask: Are shared branch prefixes duplicated or represented once?<br>Set-of-sets: each S ∈ 𝒮 stores a whole branch explicitly, so shared material may be repeated.<br>Tree view: ∧ extends a branch, ∨ splits a branch, and shared prefixes are not copied visually.<br><b>Reference:</b> Each full tree branch corresponds to one set S in the set-of-sets presentation.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Q53.</strong> What does tableau termination mean?</summary>

Use: The algorithm cannot run forever.<br>Step 1: Rules only decompose subformulae of the finite input.<br>Step 2: ∧ additions per branch are bounded by input length.<br>Step 3: ∨ branchings are finite, though exponential in the worst case.<br><b>Reference:</b> Termination: the tableau algorithm always stops, bounded exponentially in input length in the lecture.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Q54.</strong> What does tableau soundness mean?</summary>

Use: Trust a yes-answer.<br>Step 1: If the algorithm returns yes, take a final clash-free set S.<br>Step 2: Build a valuation from the literals in S.<br>Step 3: Show all formulae in S are true under that valuation.<br>Step 4: Since the input formula remains on the branch, it is satisfiable.<br><b>Reference:</b> Soundness: if tableau returns yes, then φ is satisfiable.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Q55.</strong> What does tableau completeness mean?</summary>

Use: Trust that satisfiable inputs will not be missed.<br>Step 1: Assume some valuation v makes φ true.<br>Step 2: Follow a branch that preserves truth under v.<br>Step 3: For ∧, both conjuncts stay true.<br>Step 4: For ∨, follow a true disjunct branch.<br>Step 5: The watched final branch is clash-free, so tableau returns yes.<br><b>Reference:</b> Completeness: if φ is satisfiable, tableau returns yes.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Q56.</strong> How do termination, soundness, and completeness fit together?</summary>

Step 1: Termination gives an answer eventually.<br>Step 2: Soundness says every yes-answer is correct.<br>Step 3: Completeness says every satisfiable input gets a yes-answer.<br>Step 4: Together they make tableau a decision procedure for PL satisfiability.<br><b>Reference:</b> A correct terminating yes/no algorithm is a decision procedure.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Q57.</strong> What is a decision procedure?</summary>

Use: Ask whether the algorithm always gives the correct yes/no answer in finite time.<br>Step 1: Check termination.<br>Step 2: Check correctness of yes/no answers for the target problem.<br>Step 3: If both hold, it decides the problem.<br><b>Reference:</b> A decision procedure is an algorithm that terminates and correctly decides a yes/no problem.

</details>

<summary><strong>Q58.</strong> Why is the basic tableau presentation not the same as a practical SAT solver?</summary>

Step 1: Recognise tableau as conceptually correct but potentially wasteful.<br>Step 2: Practical solvers choose efficient formula syntax and data structures.<br>Step 3: They use rule/branch ordering, heuristics, and learning from failed branches.<br>Step 4: These optimisations reduce waste in real instances.<br><b>Reference:</b> The lecture connects tableau to SAT solvers but notes implementation choices are crucial.

</details>

</details>

Step 1: Treat each rule/claim as a propositional formula φᵢ.<br>Step 2: Conjoin all formulae into one KB formula.<br>Step 3: Run reasoning tasks on the conjunction.<br><b>Reference:</b> KB = φ₁ ∧ φ₂ ∧ … ∧ φₙ.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Q62.</strong> How do you sanity-check a KB for consistency?</summary>

Step 1: Form the conjunction KB of all formulae.<br>Step 2: Check whether KB is satisfiable.<br>Step 3: If yes, the theory is consistent.<br>Step 4: If no, the theory is broken/self-contradictory.<br><b>Reference:</b> KB satisfiable? Yes means consistent theory; no means broken theory.

</details>

<summary><strong>Q63.</strong> How do you sanity-check a KB for non-vacuity?</summary>

<summary><strong>Q78.</strong> Why does a branch with a clash fail?</summary>

Step 1: Find p and ¬p together in the same S.<br>Step 2: Any valuation making p true makes ¬p false.<br>Step 3: Any valuation making ¬p true makes p false.<br>Step 4: No valuation can satisfy both requirements simultaneously.<br><b>Reference:</b> A clash is {p, ¬p} ⊆ S.
