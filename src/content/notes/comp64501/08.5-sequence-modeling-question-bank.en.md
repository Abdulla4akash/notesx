## Worked practice question bank

This bank focuses on the **computational task types actually covered by the sheet**: language-model probability calculations, RNN forward/gradient mechanics, embedding/token matrices, self-attention calculations, transformer component calculations, causal/masked attention, BERT-style masking, GPT-style shifting, beam/greedy decoding, and encoder-decoder cross-attention.

## Task types identified from the sheet

1. **Language-model probability tasks**
   - Use the chain rule to decompose a sequence probability.
   - Multiply conditional probabilities to score a sequence.
   - Compare or rank candidate next tokens.

2. **Next-token training setup tasks**
   - Prepend a start token.
   - Construct input/target pairs for next-word prediction.
   - Avoid future-token leakage.

3. **RNN computation tasks**
   - Compute a recurrent hidden state step by step.
   - Compute per-timestep predictions.
   - Compute total sequence error.

4. **BPTT and truncated BPTT tasks**
   - Write the gradient path through an unrolled RNN.
   - Identify local and future-loss contributions.
   - Compare full BPTT with a truncated approximation.

5. **Exploding/vanishing gradient tasks**
   - Multiply recurrent Jacobian factors through time.
   - Use eigenvalue-style reasoning to classify gradient behaviour.
   - Notice where the activation derivative changes the simple eigenvalue story.

6. **Input representation tasks**
   - One-hot encode tokens.
   - Multiply by an embedding matrix.
   - Assemble an $N \times D$ token matrix.

7. **Self-attention tasks**
   - Compute dot-product scores.
   - Apply row-wise softmax.
   - Form weighted sums of value vectors.
   - Use matrix-form attention $Y=\operatorname{softmax}(XX^\top)X$.

8. **Parameterized and scaled attention tasks**
   - Compute $Q=XW^{(q)}$, $K=XW^{(k)}$, $V=XW^{(v)}$.
   - Check matrix dimensions.
   - Compute $\operatorname{softmax}(QK^\top/\sqrt{D_k})V$.
   - Include biases by augmenting $X$ and the weight matrices.

9. **Multi-head and transformer-layer tasks**
   - Compute separate head outputs.
   - Concatenate heads.
   - Apply an output projection.
   - Track post-norm and pre-norm residual formulas.
   - Add positional encodings.

10. **Encoder/decoder/generation tasks**
    - Build BERT masked-token prediction examples.
    - Apply the 15% BERT masking rule.
    - Shift GPT inputs right for autoregressive prediction.
    - Apply causal attention masks.
    - Compare greedy search and beam search.
    - Identify query/key/value sources in cross-attention.

## Solutions — Section A

### Solution A1

**Step 1 — Recall the chain rule form.**

For a sequence $x_1,x_2,x_3$, the chain rule gives:

$$
p(x_1,x_2,x_3)=p(x_1)p(x_2\mid x_1)p(x_3\mid x_1,x_2).
$$

**Step 2 — Substitute the tokens.**

Here:

$$
x_1=\text{he},\qquad x_2=\text{likes},\qquad x_3=\text{apples}.
$$

Therefore:

$$
p(\text{he},\text{likes},\text{apples})
=
p(\text{he})p(\text{likes}\mid\text{he})p(\text{apples}\mid\text{he},\text{likes}).
$$

**Answer:**

$$
\boxed{p(\text{he})p(\text{likes}\mid\text{he})p(\text{apples}\mid\text{he},\text{likes})}
$$

=0.30\times 0.20\times 0.50\times 0.80\times 0.60.

$$
0.06\times 0.50=0.03

$$
0.03\times 0.80=0.024
$$

$$
0.024\times 0.60=0.0144
$$

**Answer:**

$$
\boxed{0.0144}
$$

### Solution B3

**Step 1 — Compute the local term.**

$$
T_2=0.4\times 2=0.8.
$$

**Step 2 — Compute the timestep-3 contribution.**

$$
T_3=0.5\times 2\times 0.3=0.3.
$$

**Step 3 — Compute the timestep-4 contribution.**

$$
T_4=0.25\times 2\times 0.4\times 0.3.
$$

First:

$$
0.25\times 2=0.5.
$$

Then:

$$
0.5\times 0.4=0.2.
$$

Then:

$$
0.2\times 0.3=0.06.
$$

So:

$$
T_4=0.06.
$$

**Step 4 — Compute full BPTT.**

$$
\frac{\partial E}{\partial h_2}=T_2+T_3+T_4=0.8+0.3+0.06=1.16.
$$

If only $T_2$ is kept:

$$
\frac{\partial E}{\partial h_2}\approx T_2=0.8.
$$

**Step 6 — Compute the dropped contribution.**

$$
1.16-0.8=0.36.
$$

**Answer:** full BPTT gives $1.16$, truncated BPTT gives $0.8$, and truncation drops $0.36$.

### Solution B4

**Step 1 — Case 1: $J=0.7$.**

$$
J^6=0.7^6.
$$

Compute powers:

$$
0.7^2=0.49,
\quad
0.7^3=0.343,
\quad
0.7^6=(0.343)^2\approx 0.1176.
$$

This shrinks the gradient, so it is **vanishing**.

**Step 2 — Case 2: $J=1.1$.**

$$
J^6=1.1^6.
$$

Compute powers:

$$
1.1^2=1.21,
\quad
1.1^3=1.331,
\quad
1.1^6=(1.331)^2\approx 1.7716.
$$

This grows the gradient, so it is **exploding**.

**Step 3 — Case 3: $J=1.0$.**

$$
J^6=1^6=1.
$$

This is approximately **stable**.

**Answer:**

| $J$ | $J^6$ | classification |
|---:|---:|---|
| 0.7 | 0.1176 | vanishing |
| 1.1 | 1.7716 | exploding |
| 1.0 | 1.0000 | stable |

### Solution D1

**Step 1 — Greedy first choice.**

Greedy search chooses the highest first-step probability.

$$
p(\text{nice})=0.60>p(\text{dog})=0.40.
$$

So greedy first chooses:

$$
\text{nice}.
$$

**Step 2 — Greedy second choice after `nice`.**

Among the listed `nice` continuations:

$$
p(\text{house}\mid\text{nice})=0.20,
\qquad
p(\text{but}\mid\text{nice})=0.10.
$$

So greedy chooses:

$$
\text{nice house}.
$$

**Step 3 — Compute full two-token probabilities.**

For `nice house`:

$$
0.60\times 0.20=0.12.
$$

For `nice but`:

$$
0.60\times 0.10=0.06.
$$

For `dog runs`:

$$
0.40\times 0.90=0.36.
$$

For `dog sleeps`:

$$
0.40\times 0.05=0.02.
$$

**Step 4 — Compare paths.**

$$
0.36>0.12>0.06>0.02.
$$

The best two-token path is:

$$
\text{dog runs}.
$$

**Step 5 — Explain beam width $B=2$.**

At step 1, beam width 2 keeps both:

$$
\text{nice},\quad \text{dog}.
$$

At step 2, it evaluates extensions from both retained hypotheses. Therefore it can find:

$$
\text{dog runs}
$$

with probability $0.36$.

**Answer:** greedy chooses `nice house`, but beam search with $B=2$ recovers `dog runs`.

### Solution D3

**Step 1 — Apply eigenvalue-only reasoning.**

The largest eigenvalue of $V_h$ is $1.2$.

Because

$$
1.2>1,
$$

eigenvalue-only reasoning predicts an exploding gradient.

**Step 2 — Include the activation derivative.**

The full recurrent Jacobian includes:

$$
\operatorname{diag}(g'(z_n))V_h.
$$

In the scalar approximation, the activation derivative contributes a factor of $0.5$.

So the effective recurrent factor is:

$$
0.5\times 1.2=0.6.
$$

**Step 3 — Raise the effective factor across 10 recurrent edges.**

$$
0.6^{10}\approx 0.0060.
$$

This is much smaller than 1.

**Step 4 — Classify the actual behaviour.**

Because the product shrinks toward zero, the gradient vanishes.

**Answer:** eigenvalue-only reasoning predicts explosion, but including the activation derivative gives an effective factor $0.6$, so over 10 edges the gradient shrinks to about $0.006$ and vanishes.

**Step 2 — Identify the more saturated row.**

When softmax saturates, small changes to non-winning scores barely affect the output. This can make gradients through the softmax very small, so learning becomes slow or unstable.

Scaling divides dot products by $\sqrt{D_k}$ before softmax. This reduces score magnitude, making the softmax less saturated and improving gradient flow.

The correct score shape is:

**Answer:** the correct score shape is $3\times 4$; the mistaken shape is $3\times 3$; the mistake loses conditioning on the encoded source sequence.

### Solution D10

**Step 1 — Check the raw token vectors.**

The two raw embeddings are identical:

$$
x_1=\begin{bmatrix}1\\0\end{bmatrix},
\qquad
x_2=\begin{bmatrix}1\\0\end{bmatrix}.
$$

Without positional encoding, the attention mechanism receives the same vector at both positions.

**Step 2 — Decide whether basic attention can distinguish the positions.**

Because the vectors are identical, their dot products with other vectors are also identical. Basic dot-product attention has no separate positional signal, so it cannot distinguish them by raw vector value alone.

**Step 3 — Add positional encoding.**

With positional encodings:

$$
\tilde{x}_1=x_1+r_1,
\qquad
\tilde{x}_2=x_2+r_2.
$$

If

$$
r_1\neq r_2,
$$

then generally:

$$
\tilde{x}_1\neq \tilde{x}_2.
$$

So the attention mechanism now receives different inputs for the two positions.

**Step 4 — Explain why parameterized projections matter.**

Once the inputs contain token plus position information, learned matrices

$$
W^{(q)},\quad W^{(k)},\quad W^{(v)}
$$

can learn different ways to use that information for querying, matching, and carrying values. Instead of forcing the same raw vector to play all three roles, parameterized attention learns separate query, key, and value spaces.

**Answer:** identical raw tokens cannot be distinguished by basic attention without positional information. Adding positional encodings gives different position-dependent inputs, and parameterized projections then learn how to use them as queries, keys, and values.
